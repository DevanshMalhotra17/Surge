01 — Know the Risks

Hidden Hazards After a Flood

The flood is gone, but it leaves behind dangers that aren't always visible. Never assume a flooded area is safe.

⚡

Electrical Hazards

Water and soil conduct electricity. Downed power lines may still be energized. Never touch any electrical equipment before utility crews confirm power is off.

Lethal Risk
🦠

Contaminated Water

Floodwater almost always contains raw sewage, industrial chemicals, and pathogens. Any skin contact requires thorough washing. Never drink tap water until your municipality declares it safe.

Lethal Risk
🏚️

Structural Damage

Flooding weakens foundations, walls, and floors. A structure can appear intact but be on the verge of collapse. Look for sagging, cracks, or shifted walls before entering.

Lethal Risk
🍄

Mold & Fungal Growth

Mold begins growing within 24–48 hours on wet surfaces. Many mold species cause serious respiratory illness. Wear N95 masks and gloves during any cleaning work.

Serious Risk
🐍

Wildlife & Pests

Floods displace snakes, rodents, and insects into unusual locations, including inside your home. Check dark areas, closets, and debris piles carefully before reaching in.

Serious Risk
💨

Gas Leaks

Floodwater can displace gas lines and fittings. If you smell gas or hear hissing upon return, leave immediately and call your gas utility from outside. Do not use switches.

Lethal Risk
02 — First Walkthrough

Safe Re-Entry Protocol

Before you clean a single thing, complete this inspection sequence. Skipping steps can be fatal.

01

Wait for Official Clearance

Do not return until local emergency management or law enforcement has declared the area safe. Even if the water looks gone, roads, utilities, and structures may still be compromised.

Check local emergency alerts, FEMA.gov, or your county's emergency management website for all-clear notices.
02

Gear Up Before Entering

Before you step inside: wear heavy rubber boots, waterproof gloves, goggles, and an N95 respirator mask. Floodwater leaves invisible contaminants on every surface.

Keep a change of clothes in your car — remove and bag your entry clothes outside before entering a clean environment.
03

Inspect the Structure from Outside First

Walk the perimeter before entering. Look for: foundation cracks, sagging roof lines, shifted walls, missing sections. If the structure looks compromised at all — do not enter. Contact a licensed structural engineer.

04

Check for Gas Before Opening Doors

Sniff from outside before opening any door. If you smell gas, do not open anything — call your gas utility immediately from a safe distance. If no smell, open windows and doors and wait 5 minutes before entering.

05

Document Everything BEFORE Cleaning

This is critical for insurance and FEMA claims. Take hundreds of photos and videos. Capture the waterline on walls, damage to floors and furniture, appliances, and the exterior. Do not move or discard anything until fully documented.

Use a dated timestamp. Cloud-backup your media immediately in case your phone is lost or damaged.
06

Do Not Restore Power Yourself

Never turn the power back on until a licensed electrician has inspected the electrical system. Even if breakers were off during the flood, water may have damaged wiring, outlets, and the panel.

03 — Mold Prevention

The Mold Clock is Ticking

Mold doesn't wait. Here's what happens if wet materials aren't dried out quickly — and what to do at each stage.

24–48hours
Mold spores begin to colonize On wet drywall, wood, carpet, and fabric. Invisible at this stage but actively growing. Remove standing water and begin drying immediately.
72hours
Visible mold appears Fuzzy patches emerge on walls and organic materials. Porous items like carpets, mattresses, and insulation cannot be saved — discard them.
1 week
Structural wood becomes affected Mold penetrates framing and subfloor. Professional remediation is now likely required. Do not bleach — it doesn't reach inside materials.
Ongoing
Health impacts escalate Chronic exposure to mold causes respiratory illness, particularly for children, elderly, and those with asthma. Do not occupy a moldy space. Call a certified mold remediation specialist (IICRC-certified).

05 — Get Help

Financial Assistance & Resources

You may be entitled to significant financial assistance. These are the programs to apply for immediately.

🏛️

FEMA Disaster Assistance

Even if you have insurance, FEMA may cover unmet needs: temporary housing, home repairs, medical costs, and personal property losses in federally declared disaster areas.

DisasterAssistance.gov
📋

SBA Disaster Loans

Low-interest disaster loans for homeowners, renters, and businesses to repair or replace property. You don't need to be a business owner to apply — homeowners qualify too.

SBA Disaster Loans
❤️

American Red Cross

Provides emergency shelter, food, and supplies. Case workers can help connect you to local recovery resources and offer financial assistance for immediate needs.

RedCross.org
🏠

National Flood Insurance

If you have an NFIP policy, file your claim immediately. Your insurer has 60 days to pay or deny. Document everything before moving or discarding any damaged property.

FloodSmart.gov

Your Mental Health Matters Too

Flood recovery is exhausting and emotionally traumatic. Anxiety, depression, and PTSD after natural disasters are extremely common and nothing to be ashamed of. Reach out to SAMHSA's free 24/7 Disaster Distress Helpline — trained counselors are available now.
